Introducing beach rugby

Beach rugby is a “leisure” form of rugby

  • simplified rules → low threshold
  • amateur and semi-professional teams (from beginners to ex-pros)
  • different level of tactical and technical training

Play ground

  • a sand surface
  • the field of play is 31 × 25 metres
  • 2 in-goal areas, 3 metres in lenght each
  • playing area = field of play + in-goal area × 2
  • goal lines ∈ in-goal area and ∉ field of play
  • touch lines ∉ field of play
  • dead ball and touch-in-goal lines ∉ in-goal area
  • the centre is not marked
  • there is no half way line

Schematic representation of the playing area

Dead ball and try

Try

  • When a player grounds the ball in the opponents’ in-goal area it is a try.
  • The team that scored more tries wins.

Dead ball

The ball is dead when the referee blows the whistle to stop play.

  • the ball is in touch or touch in-goal
  • the ball is grounded in in-goal
  • a try has been scored
  • the ball or ball-carrier touches the dead-ball line or anything beyond it
  • falls, knock-ons

In-goal areas are highlighted in red

Participants

  • Players
    • 2 teams
    • 5 players a side on the playing area
    • up to 7 substitutes
  • Match officials
    • the referee (within playing area)
    • 2 assistant referees (on touch lines)
    • (optional) reserve referee/assistant
    • (optional) time-keeper

Substitutions

Law 3. Number of players – the team

3.1 Each team must have no more than five players on the playing area.

3.2 …As soon as the referee knows that a team has too many players, the referee must order the captain of that team to reduce the number appropriately. A try is disallowed when scored by a team with more than the permitted number of players.

3.3 …A team may substitute any number of players during a match providing they do so when the ball is dead. Substitutes must enter the playing area at the half way line. A player leaving the playing area may do so from any place.

Observations

  • Participants express their interpretations of the laws through actions (fragments 2 and 3)
  • Officials sometimes tolerate violations of the laws (fragment 3). Sometimes they do not (fragment 2)
  • Players project a signal of a referee. They often begin to leave the playing area before the referee blows the whistle (fragments 1 and 3)
  • Participants account troubles and fix them (fragments 2 and 3)
